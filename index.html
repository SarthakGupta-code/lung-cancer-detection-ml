When my grandmother got cancer in India and we took her to the hospital for treatment, I realized how lucky we were to afford care, even if it was barely manageable. At the same time, I saw how many people simply couldn't get help.

That moment sparked a question: what if cancer could be detected early, before treatment becomes so difficult? What if a simple set of symptoms‚Äîrecorded during a routine intake‚Äîcould flag risk before it's too late?

The Problem

India lacks ground-level lung cancer data‚Äîmost ML research uses Western datasets

In India, lung cancer is diagnosed at advanced stages in the vast majority of cases. The 5-year survival rate sits at just 5%‚Äîamong the lowest globally. But here's the deeper problem: most machine learning studies on lung cancer detection use datasets from Western populations. Indian patients, with different demographic patterns, environmental exposures, and healthcare access realities, are underrepresented in the very research meant to help them.

This study takes a different approach: primary data collection from an Indian hospital, building a model trained on patients it's meant to serve.

The Approach

Ground-level fieldwork, not downloaded datasets

01

Primary Data Collection

Collected comprehensive symptom data from 2,512 patients at Neera Hospital, Lucknow‚Äîoriginal fieldwork in a real clinical setting, not a pre-existing Kaggle dataset.

02

India-Specific Features

16 clinical attributes capturing patterns specific to Indian patients: smoking habits, family exposure to secondhand smoke, occupational hazards, pollution levels, and symptom presentations.

03

Rigorous Model Comparison

Systematic evaluation of 8 ML algorithms‚ÄîLogistic Regression, Decision Trees, KNN, Random Forest, XGBoost, Gradient Boosting, CatBoost, and AdaBoost‚Äîto find the optimal approach.

04

Clinical Integration

Risk-scoring model integrated into nurse intake workflow at Neera Hospital's cancer wing‚Äîgenerating risk assessments during routine pre-examination checks.

Key Findings

What this India-specific data revealed

  • Secondhand smoke exposure increases risk 1.7√ó even in non-smokers‚Äîcritical in Indian households where family smoking is common
  • Family history of cancer correlates with 80% higher probability‚Äîgenetic factors underexplored in Indian populations
  • Ensemble methods significantly outperformed single classifiers on this dataset
  • Novel contribution: One of few recent ML studies using primary Indian patient data for lung cancer detection
